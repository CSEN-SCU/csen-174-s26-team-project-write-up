# Voice-preserving coaching

Good feedback names what the reader experiences without forcing a single “correct” style. Prefer pointing at **tradeoffs**: clarity vs. warmth, density vs. breath, formality vs. authenticity.

When writing sounds like speech, the goal is usually not to make it sound like an essay. It is to make the **through-line** easy to follow: one main move per sentence, obvious connections between ideas, and intentional repetition instead of accidental repetition.

# Coherence without rewriting

Coherence problems often come from **missing handrails**: the reader cannot tell what “this/that/it” refers to, or the order of ideas does not match the order of thought. Fixes can be tiny: add a short orienting phrase, split a long sentence, or move one clause earlier.

# Teaching patterns

If the same issue shows up across drafts, treat it as a **pattern** to practice (for example: long sentences, vague referents, stacked clauses). One-off typos are less valuable as learning targets than recurring structure habits.
